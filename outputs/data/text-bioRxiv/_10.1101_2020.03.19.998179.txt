Abstract:

24

We present the first genetic characterization of a COVID-19 cluster in Europe using

25

metagenomic next-generation sequencing (mNGS). Despite low viral loads, the mNGS

26

workflow used herein allowed to characterize the whole genome sequences of SARS-CoV2

27

isolated from an asymptomatic patient, in 2 clinical samples collected 1 day apart.

28

Comparison of these sequences suggests viral evolution with development of quasispecies. In

29

addition, the present workflow identified a new deletion in nsp2 (Asp268Del) which was

30

found in all 3 samples originating from this cluster as well as in 37 other viruses collected in

31

England and in Netherlands, suggesting the spread of this deletion in Europe. The impact of

32

Asp268Del on SARS-CoV-2 transmission and pathogenicity, as well as on PCR performances

33

and anti-viral strategy should be rapidly evaluated in further studies.

To the Editor,

35

In December 2019, a novel coronavirus emerged in China, causing outbreaks of unexplained

36

pneumonia [1]. The virus was subsequently identified as a beta-coronavirus and named severe

37

acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2). SARS-CoV-2 is responsible of the

38

coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19) pandemic which includes asymptomatic, upper, and

39

lower respiratory tract infections. Among the first European cases of COVID-19, 6 were

40

associated with a cluster of transmission in the French Alps in late January 2020 [2]. The

41

index case of this cluster travelled from Singapore to France and went back to the United

42

Kingdom (UK) where he was tested positive for SARS-CoV-2 on February 6th. Here, we

43

aimed to investigate the French cases related to this cluster using metagenomic next-

44

generation sequencing analysis (mNGS).

45

Of the 6 contact patients tested positive for SARS-CoV2, the 3 samples with highest viral

46

loads (assessed by RT-PCR targeting the RdRp gene) were selected for mNGS [3]. One

47

nasopharyngeal swab was collected from a patient with an upper respiratory tract infection on

48

February 7th (sample #1, Ct = 31.3). The other two samples were collected from the same

49

asymptomatic patient on February 8th (sample #2, nasopharyngeal swab, Ct = 31.1) and 9th

50

(sample #3, nasopharyngeal aspirate, Ct = 28.8).

51

A previously described mNGS protocol was used but for which DNase treatment was

52

performed after nucleic acid extraction in order to increase sensitivity of RNA viruses’

53

detection [4]. Low quality and human reads were filtered out and remaining reads were

54

aligned to the SARS-CoV-2 reference genome (isolate Wuhan-Hu-1, EPI_ISL_402125) using

55

BWA-MEM algorithm. A mean of 19 445 767 reads per sample were generated, of which a

56

mean of 605 243 reads per sample were mapped to the SARS-CoV-2 reference genome.

57

Percentage of genome covered at a minimum depth of coverage of 100X was 38.3% for

58

sample #1, 99.6% for sample #2 and 80.6% for sample #3.

2

59

The whole genome sequence (WGS) generated from sample #2 was deposited on GISAID

60

(EPI_ISL_410486). The phylogenetic analysis using the 571 WGS of SARS-CoV-2 publicly

61

available (as of March 17th 2020) found that this sequence clustered with a sequence collected

62

in Jiangsu, China (EPI_ISL_408488) on January 19th, suggesting a direct introduction of the

63

virus from Asia, as suggested by epidemiological data (Fig 1). Compared to the reference

64

SARS-CoV-2 sequence, a 3-nucleotide deletion in open reading frame 1ab (ORF1ab) at

65

position 1607-1609 was identified. This deletion was found in 100% of the reads at this

66

position with a coverage of 1745X around the deletion. Importantly, this deletion was also

67

identified in 100% of the reads of sample #1 and sample #3 with a coverage of 54X and

68

481X, respectively. Using CoV-GLUE resource, we found that this mutation leads to a

69

deletion of amino acid 268 in non-structural protein 2 (nsp2) [5]. Up to March 17th, this

70

deletion in nsp2 (Asp268Del) was also characterized in 37/571 (6.1%) WGS (England n = 6;

71

Netherlands n = 31). WGS-based phylogenetic analysis found that 15 viruses containing this

72

specific deletion were closed to viruses collected in China between December 2019 and early

73

February 2020, while 23 viruses collected in the Netherlands in March 2020 have slightly

74

diverged (Fig 1).

75

SARS-CoV2 sequences were not further compared between the 2 patients due to largely

76

incomplete coverage of SARS-CoV2 genome in sample #1. Nonetheless, the longitudinal

77

samples from the asymptomatic patient (sample #2 vs sample #3) were compared using a

78

minimum depth of coverage of 100X in order to make a preliminary assessment of intra-host

79

genetic variability. Three SNVs were noticed between the 2 samples: C366A (nsp1: S34Y),

80

A20475G (synonymous mutation in nsp15), T24084A (protein S: L841H), suggesting intra-

81

host evolution of the virus. For all 3 positions, nucleotides from sample #2 were still detected

82

in sample #3 but as minor variants.

In this short report, we present the first genetic characterization of a COVID-19 cluster in

84

Europe. Despite low viral loads, the mNGS workflow used herein allowed to characterize the

85

whole genome sequences of SARS-CoV2 isolated from an asymptomatic patient, in 2 clinical

86

samples collected 1 day apart. Comparison of these sequences suggests viral evolution with

87

development of quasispecies. Specific studies using high depth of coverage to assess

88

quasispecies are needed to characterize potential intra-host adaptation. In addition, the present

89

workflow identified a new deletion in nsp2 (Asp268Del) which was found in all 3 samples

90

originating from this cluster as well as in 37 other viruses collected in England (February) and

91

in Netherlands (March), suggesting the spread of this deletion in Europe. The impact of

92

Asp268Del on SARS-CoV-2 transmission and pathogenicity, as well as on PCR performances

93

and anti-viral strategy should be rapidly evaluated in further studies.
